    History of antiviruses

    A little bit about the history of antiviruses...





    The first computer virus


    The first computer virus, known as the Creeper, infected and spread to PDP-10 mainframe computers built by Digital Equipment Corporation in 1971. (DEC). Ray Tomlinson, an American computer programmer, created the Reaper to confront the Creeper. It was a program that was supposed to get rid of the virus. However, it should be remembered that the Reaper was not a true antivirus. It was, in fact, a virus, although one with a more charitable intent: to eliminate the Creeper from victim systems.

    The first antivirus Software


    The late 1980s were to be watershed years in the antivirus software industry, laying the groundwork for the entire industry. Bernd Robert Fix, a German computer security expert, developed a tool to remove Vienna, a virus that infected.com files on DOS-based systems, in 1987. Although the specific origins of the first antivirus program are unknown, this was the first documented eradication of a computer virus by an antivirus program. G Data Software AG, a German business, launched the first antivirus software for Atari ST computers around the same time. The Ultimate Virus Killer (UVK) 2000 was the company's follow-up. McAfee, Inc., a cybersecurity conglomerate, was created in 1987 as well, and it would release the first version of VirusScan by the end of the year. In 1987, Peter Pako, Miroslav Trnka, and Rudolf Hrub developed the original NOD32 Antivirus. By the end of 1987, two more antivirus programs had been released: Ross Greenberg's FlushShot Plus and Erwin Lanting's Anti4us. Despite the fact that these products are no longer available, they are regarded as the first heuristic antivirus softwares. Heuristic refers to the ability to solve issues and find answers faster than traditional or customary procedures.

    Today's computer antiviruses


    The antivirus sector made even more improvements in the 2000s. F-Secure, a firm based in Finland, released BlackLight, an anti-rootkit program, in 2005. They were the first company to do so in the field of cybersecurity. McAfee introduced Artemis in 2008, a cloud-based anti-malware feature that was included in the year's edition of McAfee VirusScan. In 2011, AVG followed suit, releasing Protective Cloud Technology, a similar concept. All of the advances from the 1980s to the present have surely contributed to cybersecurity's current status as a powerhouse business.Cybersecurity is a big business that makes billions of dollars in revenue each year, and its growth shows no signs of stopping anytime soon. Avast Software, the current market leader with a share of 19.11%, made $714 million in revenue in 2016. ESET is second in market share with 14.49% and has an estimated revenue of around $456 million each year. Avast is third with 9.57% share, and the company earned $1.75 billion in revenue in 2015.

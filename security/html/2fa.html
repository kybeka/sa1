    2FA (Two-Factor Authentication)

    Two-factor authentication (2FA) is a specific type of multi-factor authentication (MFA) that restricts access security requiring two methods (also known as authentication factors) to verify your identity. These authentication factors, for example can be something you know: like a username and password; plus something you have, like a smartphone app to approve authentication requests.

    Explanation

    Given how readily cybercriminals can steal login details;Â it's no surprise that company data breaches occur frequently. If they do, two-factor authentication is the most effective approach to keep your sensitive data safe. Two-factor authentication (2FA) protects your online accounts by adding an extra layer of security.

    To get account access, you'll need a second login credential in addition to your username and password, and getting that second credential will require access to anything you own.

    It is almost impossible to enter the account without this additional access method, making it impossible for hackers to gain access to your account using only stolen passwords and login information.



    Types of Factor Authentication

    Someone can be authenticated in a variety of ways, including using several authentication methods. The vast majority of authentication methods rely on some knowledge factors, such as a password, but as we sai befpre the two-factor authentication approaches include even a possession or an inherence component.

    For example we have:

    • A knowledge factor that is something the user knows, such as a password or a PIN (personal identification number)


    • A possession factor that is something the user has, such as an ID card, a security token, or a smartphone app, to approve authentication requests.


    • A biometric factor , also known as an inherence factor, that is the user's physical self; physical characteristics, such as fingerprints, facial recognition or voice recognition.


    • A location factor denoted by the location from which an authentication attempt is being made. For example it can be limit the authentication attempts to specific devices in a specific area or by tracking the source of an authentication attempt based on the geopositioning informations, such as the GPS (Global Positioning System) data, derived from the user's mobile phone or other device.


    • A time factor that restricts user authentication in a specific time window in which logging on is permitted.

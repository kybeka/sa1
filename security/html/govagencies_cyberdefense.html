    CYBER THREATS AND PUBLIC INSTITUTIONS

    The digital world has transformed our lives creating new ways of communicating, organizing and accessing information but it has also created new threats popularly known as cyber threats. In response cyberspace is increasingly being framed as something dangerous which requires more scrutiny, management and control.









    THE PUBLIC MANAGEMENT PERSPECTIVE

    In media and policy spaces cyber threats are discussed under the names: data breaches, hacks, cyber incidents and cyber attacks. In Turkey hundreds of people have been investigated by the cyber crime unit for insulting the president. In this example a cyber threat can be taken to activities which simply involve the use of an information technology. This blurs the line between activities which affect the integrity of online systems and networks and activities which simply involve the Internet as a medium. With cyber crime increasingly overlapping with a range of policy debates defining where these boundaries sit is only going to become more unclear. Everyday life will suffer since major public services and businesses depend on the security of networks and systems, without proper measures in place the huge amounts of sensitive information we share about ourselves is vulnerable to misuse. At the same time securitized a ban on encryption (i.e.) in the name of terrorism and cyber crime, doesn't just damage human rights like privacy and freedom of expression it weakens security too by creating more vulnerabilities in systems. Something like a backdoor is a backdoor to anyone, whether that's a policeman or a criminal so many of our rights depend on our networked information infrastructure. Governments are developing norms of responsible state behavior collaborating with other countries like the bilateral agreements signed between the US and China which attempt to find rules of procedure and mutual notification systems in case of an attack. NATO also charged an independent academia to develop an international law for peace and wartime in the digital age and the results are written in the Tallinn Manual 2.0. Most countries have also established computer emergency response teams which help identify, isolate and prevent against viruses, hacking or DDoS attacks.

    THE PUBLIC MANAGEMENT PERSPECTIVE

    The U.S. personnel department was hacked in 2015 exposing the sensitive personal information of 22 million people including mental health records and details of drug and alcohol abuse. This was a catastrophe either for human rights and security terms demanding urgent action to strengthen protections. This didn't happen, instead states started to blame each other so there was any real debate on an appropriate response which might have included a discussion on whether so much data should have been collected in the first place. This is an example of what can happen when policy discussions are led by security agenda. Another case worth notice is pakistan's prevention of electronic crimes bill which has been under negotiation since 2015. It aims to “fight” crime and terrorism by restricting the ability of the actors to organize and communicate online. To do this they seek to criminalize the production, distribution and use of encryption tools.

    THE PUBLIC MANAGEMENT PERSPECTIVE

    These previous example shows a disproportionate measure in response: no distinction between the tiny number of users who might use anonymity for criminal purposes and the vast majority of users who want just to protect their privacy. We can already see how this procedures threatens also free speech by criminalizing any ambiguous content. This ineffective approach to fighting crime threatens online security and data protection and undermines free expression. To find a midway solution we must raise awareness of the dangers that come. We can encourage and support businesses that follow human rights and data protection standards. We should then intervene in spaces for debates to reach more proportionate solutions. Governments has to put in place adequate legislation and policies which put responsible limits on the power of intelligence and law enforcement agencies.

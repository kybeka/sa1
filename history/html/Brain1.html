    1986 - Brain Computer Virus

    IBM Personal Computer

    The IBM Personal Computer was the first computer in IBM's PC model line, launched on August 12, 1981, and characterized by an open architecture and a rapidly growing market for third-party support of peripherals, expansion cards, and software. The PC had a major impact on the personal computer market: the IBM PC specification became one of the most popular computer design standards in the world, and the only major competition from incompatible platforms in the 1980s was Apple's Macintosh line. Most of today's PCs are distant descendants of the IBM PC.

    Three operating systems (OS) were available for it. The cheapest and most popular was Microsoft's PC-DOS. A crucial concession was that the agreement with IBM allowed Microsoft to sell its own version, MS-DOS, for non-IBM computers. The only element of the original PC architecture that belonged exclusively to IBM was the BIOS (Basic Input/Output System).

    Brain's Origin

    Brain was written by Amjad Farooq Alvi and Basit Farooq Alvi, who at the time lived in Chah Miran, in Lahore, Pakistan. The brothers told Time magazine that they wrote it to safeguard their medical software against unlawful copying, and that it was only meant to be used for that purpose. The Brain was never intended to delete data or even slow down machines in their original form. It was only a way for Amjad and his brother to track who was using their program. The Alvi brothers moved on to focus on their internet service and telecommunication applications after Brain was rapidly followed by far more destructive programs.

    A copy of that floppy disk somehow made its way to the United States. The virus, which self-replicated and copied itself onto the disks, spread like wildfire. According to some estimates, the Brain Virus infected over 100,000 computers between 1986 and 1989, with 10,000 of them at Georgetown University in Washington, DC.

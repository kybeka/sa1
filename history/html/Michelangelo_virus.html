    1991 - Michelangelo Virus

    The Michelangelo virus is a computer virus first discovered in 4th February 1991 in Australia. The virus was designed to infect DOS systems. Michelangelo, it's like all boot sector viruses, basically operated at the BIOS level. Each year, the virus remained dormant until March 6, the birthday of Rena issance artist Michelangelo. There is no reference to the artist in the virus, and it is doubtful that the virus writer intended Michelangelo to be substantiated to the virus. Michelangelo is a variant of the already endemic Stoned virus. Only 10,000 to 20,000 cases of data loss were reported.[1]

    On March 6, if the PC is an AT or a PS/2, the virus overwrites first one hundred sectors of the hard disk with nulls. The user's data would still be on the hard fragment, it would be irretrievable for the average user.

    What was the impact of this virus?

    - On hard disks, the virus moves the original master boot record to cylinder 0, head 0, sector 7.

    - On floppy disks, if the fragment is 360 KB, the virus moves the original boot sector to cylinder 0, head 1, sector3.

    - On other disks, the virus moves the original boot sector to cylinder 0, head 1, sector 14.

    At its height, the Michelangelo virus gained notoriety for its apparently large-scale impact.

    Antivirus colonist John McAfee estimeted that the malware could have infected a numerous as 5 million computers, which was a significant amount in the early 90's.

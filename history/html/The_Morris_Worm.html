    1988 - The Morris Worm

    Why and how?

    Robert Morris was interessed to learn the potential of computers and to learn he thought to develop a virus. In fact the virus was created only to understand if it could be developed or not.

    The worm exploits various vulnerabilities to gain access to certain systems, for example a hole in the debug mode of the Unix application sendmail, a buffer overflow or a hole in the digital network service. The worm was programmed to check each computer it found to determine if the infections was already present.

    To develop the worm, Morris, concealed his activities by hacking into the MIT network to launch his worm, attempting to cover his tracks but one of his friends tell to the New York Times the plan.

    Causes and Damages

    Clifford Stoll , a system administrator, helped fight the worm and in 1989 he wrote that found 2'000 computers that was been infected within 15 hours and removing the virus can took 2 days.

    The Morris Worm is called the Great worm due the devastating effect it had on the internet at that time.

    The virus possessed a capability at its core that was perhaps the most problematic, namely, the virus's ability to reinfect the computer once its infection was eradicated. If the computer had already been infected, the chances of infection returning were 1 in 7 times. This was done to hasten the virus's transmission and prevent a user from imitating a worm process to fake an infection in order to avoid being infected. The virus is said to have affected around 6'000 systems in all, which is three times what Clifford predicted.

    The average of the cost of the damage, valued from the U.S. Governament Accountability Office , is between $100'000 - $10'000'000.

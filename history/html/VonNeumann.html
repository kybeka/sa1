    1966 - Theory of Self-Reproducing Automata

    John von Neumann

    John von Neumann, born Janos Neumann, was a Hungarian-American polymath, generally regarded as the foremost mathematician of his time. His contribution extends into many different fields, such as mathematics with his research on analysis, physics with his theory on quantum mechanics and computer science with his Von Neumann architecture and his studies on linear programming, self-replicating machines and stochastic computing.

    In his lifetime, Von Neumann produced about 150 papers, with around 60 in pure mathematics, 60 in applied mathematics, 20 in physics, and the rest on specific mathematical or non-mathematical themes. His final effort, an incomplete manuscript produced while in the hospital, was ultimately released as The Computer and the Brain as a book.

    Theory and Organization of Complicated Automata

    In this essay, later published as "Theory of self-reproducing automata", John Von Neumann described how a computer program could be designed to reproduce itself. This desgin for a self-reproducing computer program is considered as the world's first computer virus. Von Neumann's analysis of the structure of self-replication preceded the discovery of the structure of DNA and heavily influenced the successive researches on computer viruses.

    Bell Labs employees gave life to von Neumann's theory in the 1950s in a game they called "Core Wars." Later redesigned and published by D. G. Jones and A. K. Dewdney in 1984, Core War became a popular game in which two or more battle programs compete for control of a virtual computer.
